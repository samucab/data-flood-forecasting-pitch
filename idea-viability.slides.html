pitched by Samuel Barsanelli Costa | Data Science batch #1643

1.1. Problem statement¶

What is an urban flood and why should we care about forecasting it?

A picture is worth a thousand words...

This is a global issue related to climate change, as raised by the UN's GAR23 report:

  • the increase in the intensity of rainfall is already increasing flood risk
  • flood damage shall increase by 170% with a global 2C warming

Additional regional conditions may intensify this effects, such as the El Nio that triggered unprecedented flooding in the southern Brazilian state of Rio Grande do Sul.

With the best think globally, act locally spirit in mind, this idea is scoped to predict flood in (yet to be defined) one among the most affected cities at the Porto Alegre Metro Area during the flooding events of April and May 2024

From a data science perpective, flood prediction is not new. The Journal of Hydrology alone has over 5k publications about it over the last 20 years. And the vast majority of them uses RNN-LSTM models for such predictions.

Even Google has it's own AI model to predict daily flood. They've published their approach on a paper in Nature, and made it online at the Flood Hub in 80+ countries.

But all resources available about flooding are way too much technical, not suited for the mainstream.

Could anyone quickly disgest this data plot and make an informed "fight-or-flight" decision?

The driving questions of this idea are:

  • can a classification model be trained to predict flood from rainfall data?
  • how accurate could it be?
  • how for in advance could we predict a flood (e.g. 5-day)?
  • could we publish it in real-time in a easy-to-digest fashion?
  • does it make sense to communicate just as we do with weather forecast?

1.2. Viability analysis¶

To address this model we'll need, at least:

  • river stage data: the measure of how high the level of the river is, at a certain location, in reference to a ground level (usually measured in cm). This is the target variable, the y
  • historical rainfall data: the amount of rainfall at a certain location, measured as the amont of rainfall in a squared meter and reported as water height of that square (in mm). This is the exogenous variable, our X to train/test the model.
  • future rainfall data: this should be a reliable rainfall forecast source, in the same format as the historical data (measuring unit and sampling rate). This is our X to predict the future.

Automated Telemetry Stations comes out to the the best source for this goal, as they continuosly collect data at a certain timeframe (usually between 15 min and 60 min) and is less prone to human data collection errors.

Relevant info regarding rain data sourcing

  • Rainfall is a spatially distributed fenomenon.
  • All rain from up in the mountains will, at some point in time, flow over the hills and hit the lowest point (it might take hours or even days).
  • So the more rainfall stations we collect data from within the contributing are, the best!
